Feature Definitions

Every input variable fed to the XGBoost sub-models — what it is clinically, how it is derived from DiNC data, and why it matters for risk prediction.

Status legend

Vitals + Labs
source: BP readings · lab results in DiNC
All continuous values are z-scored: z = (value − μ) / σ, where μ and σ are computed from the training set and stored as artefacts. Raw values are separately retained for hard rule evaluation.
bp_systolic_z
mmHg → z-score
required hard rule
Systolic blood pressure, normalised. The top number in a BP reading — pressure when the heart beats. Elevated values are a primary maternal risk signal.
+ expand
Clinical meaning
Systolic BP is the peak pressure in your arteries when the heart contracts. Normal ≈ 120 mmHg. Sustained elevation in pregnancy causes damage to blood vessels in the placenta and maternal organs. It is the primary signal for pre-eclampsia and hypertensive crisis.
Example values Normal: 118 mmHg → z = −0.13
Elevated: 140 mmHg → z = +1.33
Critical: 165 mmHg → z = +3.00 — hard rule M1 fires
Hard rule thresholds (raw value)
M1: BP ≥ 160 → Tier 1 immediately (any trimester)
M2: BP ≥ 140 AND trimester 3 → Tier 1
N3: BP ≥ 160 in diabetic patient → Tier 1
Sub-model use Present in SM1, SM2 (maternal) and SM3, SM4 (NCD). In maternal models, BP interacts with trimester — the same elevation is higher risk in T3 than T1.
bp_diastolic_z
mmHg → z-score
required hard rule
Diastolic blood pressure, normalised. The bottom number — pressure when the heart rests. Used alongside systolic because the combination is more informative than either alone.
+ expand
Clinical meaning
Diastolic BP reflects vascular resistance — how hard the heart works to push blood through. Normal ≈ 80 mmHg. Diastolic ≥ 90 in pregnancy is a diagnostic criterion for gestational hypertension alongside systolic ≥ 140.
Why both systolic and diastolic Systolic 138 + diastolic 92 = borderline pre-eclampsia.
Systolic 155 + diastolic 75 = isolated systolic hypertension (different risk profile).
The model captures this interaction via tree splits.
Clinical reference ranges
Normal: < 80 mmHg
Elevated: 80–89 mmHg
Stage 1 hypertension: 90–99 mmHg
Stage 2 hypertension: ≥ 100 mmHg
hba1c_z
% → z-score
required hard rule
Glycated haemoglobin, normalised. Reflects average blood glucose over 2–3 months — cannot be gamed by fasting before the test. Dominant signal in NCD sub-models.
+ expand
Clinical meaning
When blood glucose is elevated, glucose attaches to haemoglobin. HbA1c measures the percentage of haemoglobin that has been glycated over the lifespan of a red blood cell (~90 days). Unlike a glucose reading, it reflects long-term control — a patient cannot temporarily improve it by fasting before the test.
Diagnostic thresholds Normal: < 5.7%
Prediabetes: 5.7–6.4%
Diabetes: ≥ 6.5% (ADA criterion)
Poorly controlled: ≥ 8.0%
Very poorly controlled: ≥ 10.0% → hard rule N1 fires
Why it matters here
In NCD sub-models (SM3/SM4), hba1c_z is typically the highest-SHAP feature. A patient on medication with HbA1c still at 9.5 is not responding to treatment. In maternal models it is optional — gestational diabetes is screened differently, but an existing diabetic who is pregnant needs both branches active.
glucose_z
mg/dL → z-score
required hard rule
Fasting blood glucose, normalised. Measured after 8 hours without food. Complements HbA1c — snapshot today vs 3-month average. Carries more weight when HbA1c is unavailable.
+ expand
Clinical meaning
Blood glucose after an overnight fast. Above-normal values indicate insufficient insulin action. Unlike HbA1c, this reflects the patient's current metabolic state rather than the 3-month average.
Reference ranges Normal: < 100 mg/dL
Prediabetes: 100–125 mg/dL
Diabetes: ≥ 126 mg/dL (WHO criterion)
Hyperosmolar risk: ≥ 300 mg/dL → hard rule N2 fires
Rural PHC relevance
In many PHC settings, a glucometer is more available than HbA1c testing equipment. In SM4 (NCD, no medication) where HbA1c may be unavailable, fasting glucose often becomes the primary glycaemic signal. XGBoost handles the case where only one of these two features is present.
haemoglobin_z
g/dL → z-score
optional hard rule
Blood haemoglobin concentration, normalised. Measures oxygen-carrying capacity of blood. Severe anaemia in pregnancy is a significant maternal mortality risk factor.
+ expand
Clinical meaning
Haemoglobin carries oxygen from lungs to tissues. In pregnancy, demand increases significantly. Anaemia means insufficient oxygen delivery to both mother and fetus. Severe anaemia (Hb < 7 g/dL) leads to cardiac stress, increased risk during delivery, and fetal growth restriction.
Reference ranges (women) Normal: 12–16 g/dL
Mild anaemia: 10–11.9 g/dL
Moderate: 7–9.9 g/dL
Severe: < 7 g/dL → hard rule M5 fires
Why optional
Not every PHC has a haemoglobinometer at every visit. When present, it is especially informative in SM2 (maternal, no medication) — a patient with no prescription records but severely low Hb is at acute risk without any drug adherence data to signal it. The model weighs it heavily when it exists.
bmi_z
kg/m² → z-score (clipped ±3σ)
required
Body Mass Index, normalised and clipped at ±3σ. Interacts with BP and glucose — high BMI compounds both. Clipped to prevent data entry errors from dominating predictions.
+ expand
Clinical meaning
BMI = weight (kg) / height (m)². A measure of weight relative to height. High BMI in pregnancy is associated with gestational diabetes, hypertension, delivery complications. Low BMI (underweight <18.5) is also a risk — associated with preterm birth and low birth weight.
Reference ranges Underweight: < 18.5
Normal: 18.5–24.9
Overweight: 25–29.9
Obese: ≥ 30
Why clip at ±3σ
Rural PHC data entry often has errors — weight recorded in lbs when the field expects kg, or height in cm when expecting metres. These produce extreme BMI values (e.g. 800) that would dominate every tree split. Clipping at ±3σ removes the influence of these outliers without affecting real high-BMI patients who cluster within 2–3σ.
creatinine_z
mg/dL → z-score
optional hard rule
Serum creatinine, normalised. A waste product filtered by kidneys — elevated levels signal renal impairment. NCD sub-models only, as diabetic nephropathy is a leading complication.
+ expand
Clinical meaning
Creatinine is a waste product of muscle metabolism, filtered by the kidneys. When kidneys are damaged (as in diabetic nephropathy), they filter less efficiently and creatinine accumulates in blood. It is the standard marker for kidney function in diabetes management.
Reference ranges (women) Normal: 0.6–1.1 mg/dL
Mildly elevated: 1.2–2.0 mg/dL
Significant impairment: > 2.0 mg/dL → hard rule N5 fires
Why optional
Renal function tests are less commonly ordered at PHC level — they typically require a laboratory rather than a point-of-care test. When available, especially for a known diabetic patient, creatinine significantly improves NCD risk stratification. Absent for most maternal-only patients.
Medication Features
source: drug name · dosage · dispensing date in DiNC EMR
Derived from the prescription and pharmacy dispensing records. The routing flag on_medication determines which of the four sub-models runs for each branch.
on_medication
boolean 0 / 1
derived
Whether the patient has any active prescription in DiNC. The routing flag — determines which sub-model runs. Derived from any drug record with last dispensed date within 90 days.
+ expand
How it is derived
Computed as: any(last_dispensed_date > today − 90 days) across all prescription records for this patient. The 90-day window is conservative — most chronic medications require collection every 30–60 days, so 90 days allows for missed collections without falsely routing to "no medication."
Why it matters
A patient on medication and a patient not on medication have structurally different feature sets. adherence_gap_days, drug_class_code, dose_change_flag, and polypharmacy_count simply do not exist for an unmedicated patient. Running both on the same model would create a highly unbalanced and noisy training set. Separate models prevent this.
drug_class_code
integer (ATC code)
required SM1/SM3 derived
ATC drug class as an integer. Drug name from DiNC EMR looked up in a district formulary table using fuzzy matching. Contextualises other lab values — the same HbA1c means different things depending on which drug the patient is on.
+ expand
How it is derived
The drug name string from DiNC (e.g. "Metformin 500mg") is matched against the district formulary using Levenshtein distance ≤ 2 (to handle spelling variants like "Metformine" or "meformin"). The match returns an ATC level-2 code (e.g. A10BA02 for biguanides), which is encoded as an integer for the model.
ATC code examples Metformin → A10BA → code 10
Insulin → A10A → code 11
ACE inhibitors → C09A → code 22
Statins → C10A → code 31
Iron supplements → B03A → code 51
Methyldopa (BP in pregnancy) → C02A → code 42
Why it matters
HbA1c of 8.5 in a patient on metformin alone means first-line therapy is not controlling the disease — moderate risk, consider escalating. HbA1c of 8.5 in a patient already on insulin + metformin means a more advanced case with treatment-resistant hyperglycaemia — higher risk. The drug class code allows the model to make this distinction.
adherence_gap_days
integer days
required SM1/SM3 derived hard rule
Days since last medication collection. Closest proxy for adherence without a pill count. Set to 999 if no dispensing record exists — not a missing value, a meaningful clinical signal.
+ expand
How it is derived
Computed as: today − last_dispensed_date. If no dispensing record exists at all (patient was prescribed but never collected), set to 999. This is not the same as null — 999 explicitly tells the model "this patient has a prescription but has never collected it," which is a distinct clinical situation from having no prescription.
Interpretation examples 0–14 days: likely adhering
15–30 days: borderline (monthly supply)
31–60 days: likely lapsed
> 60 days: high probability of non-adherence
999: never collected
Key interactions in model
In SM1 (maternal + medication), adherence_gap_days × trimester is the most informative interaction. A T3 patient who has not collected iron or methyldopa in 3 weeks is at acute risk. Hard rule N4: HbA1c ≥ 8.0 AND gap > 30 days → Tier 1, regardless of model score.
dose_change_flag
boolean 0 / 1
optional derived
Whether the patient's prescription was changed in the last 60 days. A recent dose escalation often means the prior dose was failing to control the condition.
+ expand
How it is derived
Derived by comparing the current prescription record to the previous one: if dose (mg) or drug name changed in the last 60 days, flag = 1. Absent from SM2 and SM4 (no medication records exist for these patients).
Why it matters
A dose escalation is a clinical signal that the previous regimen was inadequate. A patient whose antihypertensive was just doubled is more likely still uncontrolled than a patient stable on the same dose for 2 years. When this flag fires alongside elevated BP or HbA1c, it reinforces the risk reading.
polypharmacy_count
integer 0–10+
optional derived
Number of distinct active drug classes the patient is currently on. High polypharmacy signals complex multimorbid patients and indicates disease has progressed past first-line treatment.
+ expand
How it is derived
Count of unique ATC class codes in the patient's active prescription list (last_dispensed within 90 days). Each distinct drug class counted once, regardless of how many drugs within that class are prescribed.
Example Metformin (A10) + Insulin (A10) + Lisinopril (C09) + Atorvastatin (C10) = count 3, not 4, because metformin and insulin are the same ATC class.
Why it matters
Polypharmacy (4+ drug classes) signals a complex patient with multiple active conditions — higher risk of drug interactions, non-adherence due to pill burden, and more advanced disease. A diabetic patient on 4 drug classes has already progressed through multiple lines of treatment. This feature contextualises the severity independently of individual lab values.
ANC / PNC Features
source: antenatal care register · visit records in DiNC
Features derived from the pregnancy care record. Active only when the maternal branch is triggered (gestational_week present). Low compliance and recent visit gaps are among the strongest predictors of adverse maternal outcomes.
gestational_week
integer 1–42 weeks
required
How many weeks pregnant the patient is. Also the branching condition — if null, the maternal branch does not activate. Risk profile changes non-linearly across pregnancy, especially in T3.
+ expand
Clinical meaning
Gestational age in weeks from last menstrual period. Most acute obstetric complications — eclampsia, placental abruption, preterm labour, pre-eclampsia — cluster in the third trimester (week 27+). The model uses the raw week number (not just trimester) to distinguish week 28 from week 38 within T3.
Role in the model
Serves both as the maternal branch trigger (if null → maternal branch off) and as a direct feature in SM1 and SM2. The model learns that the same BP elevation carries different risk depending on gestational age — a BP of 142 at week 36 is an emergency; the same reading at week 10 is elevated but less acute.
trimester
integer 1 / 2 / 3
derived required
Derived from gestational_week: T1 = weeks 1–13, T2 = 14–26, T3 = 27+. Used for group-level interactions in hard rules and as a cleaner categorical signal alongside raw gestational week.
+ expand
Why both trimester and gestational_week
Gestational_week gives fine-grained risk (week 36 vs week 28 are both T3 but different acuity). Trimester gives a cleaner categorical grouping for interactions. The model can learn "T3 AND missed visits" as a pattern more easily when trimester is its own feature, rather than having to learn the threshold at week 27 from the raw number alone.
Hard rule references
M2: BP ≥ 140 AND trimester = 3 → Tier 1
M3: anc_compliance < 40% AND trimester = 3 → Tier 1
M4: anc_compliance < 50% AND trimester = 2 AND BP > 130 → Tier 1
anc_compliance
float 0–1
required derived hard rule
Ratio of ANC visits completed to visits expected at this gestational age. WHO recommends 8 contacts. Low compliance is one of the strongest predictors of adverse outcomes — unmonitored patients develop undetected complications.
+ expand
How it is derived
Formula: visits_done / visits_expected. The denominator (expected visits) is derived from the WHO ANC schedule — 8 contacts recommended across a normal pregnancy, with specific timing windows. The expected count at a given gestational week is calculated from this schedule, not from the total 8.
Examples Week 32, 4 visits done / 6 expected = 0.67 (moderate)
Week 36, 2 visits done / 7 expected = 0.29 (very low)
Week 16, 2 visits done / 2 expected = 1.00 (compliant)
Hard rule thresholds
M3: compliance < 40% AND T3 → Tier 1 (severely under-monitored in highest-risk period)
M4: compliance < 50% AND T2 AND BP > 130 → Tier 1 (elevated BP, not being monitored, still in T2)
last_visit_gap_days
integer days
required derived
Days since the patient's last visit to any DiNC facility. Captures recency — a patient with good historical compliance who then goes silent in T3 would not be caught by anc_compliance alone.
+ expand
Distinction from anc_compliance
anc_compliance is a historical ratio — it can look acceptable even if the patient dropped out recently. last_visit_gap_days captures the recency dimension. A patient with compliance = 0.75 but who has not been seen in 45 days during T3 is at high risk despite the reasonable compliance ratio.
Sub-model importance
In SM2 (maternal, no medication), this is the most important feature after BP — there is no adherence_gap signal, so visit recency becomes the primary proxy for patient engagement. In NCD sub-models (SM3, SM4), it captures whether the patient is still being seen at all, which matters for monitoring glucose and renal function over time.
high_risk_flag_prev
boolean 0 / 1
optional
Whether the patient was flagged as high-risk at any prior DiNC encounter. A strong prior — even if today's readings look better, a history of being flagged is clinically meaningful.
+ expand
How it is derived
Pulled from the patient's longitudinal record in DiNC — any prior encounter that resulted in a Tier 1 or Tier 2 classification. Optional because first-time patients on DiNC have no prior encounter history (null).
Why it matters
A patient with prior high-risk flags has a known clinical concern on record. Today's readings might temporarily appear better (white coat normotension, one normal glucose reading), but the prior flag is a signal that this patient needs continued vigilance. The model uses it as a Bayesian prior.
Derived Features
computed from multiple DiNC fields · not stored directly
Computed at preprocessing time. Encode temporal patterns that cannot be captured from any single field alone.
hba1c_delta
% per 30 days
derived optional · SM3 only
Rate of change in HbA1c per 30 days. Only computed when ≥ 2 timestamped readings exist in the last 180 days. Captures trajectory — a rising HbA1c despite medication is a different risk level from a stable one.
+ expand
Formula
(HbA1c_current − HbA1c_previous) / days_between × 30

Positive delta = worsening. Negative delta = improving. Only computed when ≥ 2 readings exist within 180 days — otherwise null, and the model runs without it.
Example Reading 1 (90 days ago): HbA1c = 7.2%
Reading 2 (today): HbA1c = 9.1%
Delta = (9.1 − 7.2) / 90 × 30 = +0.63% per month
Interpretation: worsening rapidly despite medication
Why it is the most important SM3 feature
A patient stable at HbA1c 8.0 for 12 months is poorly controlled but predictable. A patient who jumped from 6.8 to 9.1 in 3 months is deteriorating rapidly — the drug is failing, adherence may have collapsed, or a new condition is interfering. The point-in-time HbA1c value (9.1) does not tell you this. Only the trajectory does. When hba1c_delta exists, it is typically the highest-SHAP feature in SM3.
What the model does not use
Deliberately excluded — not because the data is unavailable, but because including it would introduce bias, noise, or ethical concerns.
Age
Optional in SM3/SM4 only. Excluded from maternal models — gestational_week and trimester already encode the pregnancy timeline. Age in rural maternal health correlates strongly with socioeconomic status, which would introduce confounding.
Name · ABHA ID · location
Identifying fields stripped before the feature vector is built. The model operates on numbers only. Patient identity is rejoined to the output record at the writing stage, after prediction.
Doctor notes · free text
No NLP on clinical notes in Phase 1. Free text quality in rural PHCs is inconsistent — abbreviations, local language terms, blank fields. Structured fields are the only inputs to prevent noise from degrading predictions.
Socioeconomic indicators
Income, caste, household size, and occupation are not features. Not available consistently in DiNC, and including them would make the model discriminatory — patients from lower socioeconomic backgrounds would receive higher risk scores due to confounders, not clinical risk.
